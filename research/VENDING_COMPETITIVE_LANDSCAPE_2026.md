## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Bottom line: Nobody is doing what you described.** There are companies doing *pieces* of it — age-verified vending (VapeTM), smart vending software (Cantaloupe/Nayax), curated food vending (Farmer's Fridge), and IoT-connected machines (Invenda). But **no one is combining lightweight/portable form factor + segment-curated assortment + mobile/relocatable deployment + cloud fleet management + auto-replenishment logistics** into a single platform.

The closest comparisons have either failed (Stockwell, Bodega) or are niche single-category plays (Farmer's Fridge = salads, VapeTM = vapes). The "mobile retail node" concept is an open lane.

**Pattern from the graveyard:**
1. Stockwell/Bodega died on **unit economics** — too many SKUs, not enough velocity per location
2. Reis & Irvy's died on **over-engineering** — a robot that makes fro-yo is cool but breaks constantly
3. Swyft survives but is **too expensive** — $15K+ per unit limits scale
4. Farmer's Fridge works because it's **one category, one format, high-traffic fixed locations**

**Your edge:** You're not trying to stock 200 SKUs. You're curating 15-25 items for a specific segment. That fixes the Stockwell/Bodega unit economics problem. And by going lightweight/portable, you fix the Swyft pricing problem.

## TECHNOLOGY STACK — BUY VS BUILD

| Component | Best Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Payment terminal** | Nayax VPOS Touch ($150-200) | $8/mo subscription | Industry standard. Tap/chip/QR. MDB/DEX compatible. Don't build this. |

| **Fleet management software** | **BUILD THIS** — this is your moat | Dev cost | Nayax/Parlevel charge $50-100/mo/machine for mediocre dashboards. You build better for $0 marginal cost. |

## REGULATORY LANDSCAPE — AGE-RESTRICTED VENDING

| Jurisdiction | Rule | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal (US)** | No federal ban on tobacco/nicotine vending. FDA requires age verification at point of sale. | Must have reliable age-check mechanism |
| **State-by-state** | ~20 states restrict tobacco vending to 21+ establishments (bars, clubs). Some ban entirely. Some require line-of-sight by staff. | Placement strategy must be state-aware. Bars/clubs are safest legally. |
| **College campuses** | Many campuses ban tobacco sales on-campus. Off-campus (bar districts, apartments) = fair game. | Your "college town bar district" concept threads the regulatory needle perfectly |
| **Fire stations / gov buildings** | Generally no restrictions on non-tobacco items. Tobacco/nicotine may require approval. | Mixed-assortment machines solve this — nicotine as optional add-on in permissive locations |
| **EU** | Varies by country. UK restricts tobacco vending to 18+ licensed premises. Many EU countries allow with age verification. | Expansion possible but country-by-country |
| **Age verification tech** | AI age estimation (PopCom model) not yet FDA-approved for tobacco sales. Physical ID scan or RFID/NFC required. | v1 should use ID scan. AI estimation as frictionless layer on top. |

## WHAT MAKES THIS DEFENSIBLE

1. **Segment curation knowledge** — Knowing what a 22-year-old wants at 1am vs what a firefighter wants at shift change is *expertise*, not data. That's your brain. Hard to copy.

2. **Fleet software + demand intelligence** — If you build the cloud platform that manages location, inventory, auto-restock, and demand prediction — that's your real IP. The machine is a container. The software is the business.

3. **Placement relationships** — Getting into 500 locations (bar owners, property managers, fire chiefs, gym owners) creates a distribution network that takes years to replicate. First-mover in each location wins.

4. **Regulatory moat** — If you nail age verification for regulated products, compliance becomes your barrier to entry. New entrants have to solve it from scratch.

5. **Data flywheel** — More machines → more transaction data → better predictions → better assortments → higher velocity → more placements. Classic network effect.
